# Competitive landscape: what travel companies do right and wrong

Research for **THE Travel Club** (Travel Happier Everywhere), compiled July 2026.

**Method.** Two research tracks. Track 1: a multi-agent deep-research run (106 agents; 5 search
angles, 24 sources fetched, 59 claims extracted, each surviving claim adversarially verified by a
3-skeptic vote — 22 confirmed, 3 refuted, the rest discarded). Track 2: a dedicated pass on four
named travel-club companies (arrivia, RSI Vacations, Global Discovery Vacations, Westgate Travel
Club). Every claim below carries a source; anything that could not be verified is marked.
Enforcement documents, regulator guidance, BBB pattern analyses, and peer-reviewed research
outrank review-site sentiment throughout.

## Part 1 — The verified failure patterns (big OTAs and the industry at large)

### 1. Hidden fees / drip pricing — the #1 documented failure, now illegal for lodging

The FTC's dark-patterns report identifies "burying junk fees" (advertising a partial price,
revealing mandatory charges late in checkout) as a prevalent, actionable dark pattern. The FTC's
Junk Fees Rule (16 CFR Part 464, effective May 12, 2025) requires any seller of short-term
lodging — explicitly including OTAs and platforms that display lodging prices — to show the total
price with all mandatory fees upfront. Penalties run to $51,744 per violation.
Confidence: high (3-0 verification).
Sources: [FTC dark-patterns report](https://www.ftc.gov/reports/bringing-dark-patterns-light) ·
[Morgan Lewis rule analysis](https://www.morganlewis.com/pubs/2025/01/ftc-issues-final-junk-fees-rule-to-crackdown-on-aggressive-pricing-practices) ·
[FTC rule FAQ](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/rule-unfair-or-deceptive-fees-frequently-asked-questions)

### 2. Hopper — the clearest OTA cautionary case

BBB's formal complaint-pattern analysis (2024-03-20) flagged three recurring themes: hidden
fees/unexpected charges, cancellation/refund difficulties, and unreachable customer service. In
July 2026 Hopper agreed to pay **$35 million** to settle FTC allegations that it charged hidden,
**pre-selected** fees ("Tip" and "VIP Support") without consent and excluded them from the
displayed total. The FTC settlement independently validated the exact pattern BBB had flagged two
years earlier. Hopper's standard defense — "we are bound by airline/hotel refund policies" — did
not save it. (A claim that Hopper had ~1,325 BBB complaints was refuted in verification; exact
volume is unverified.)
Confidence: high (3-0).
Sources: [BBB complaint pattern](https://www.bbb.org/us/ma/boston/profile/travel-services/hopper-usa-inc-0021-164791/complaints) ·
[FTC press release](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2026/07/travel-app-hopper-pay-35-million-settle-ftc-allegations-it-charged-fees-without-consent-deceived) ·
[TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/02/travel-app-hopper-to-pay-35m-in-ftc-settlement-over-unfairly-charging-hidden-fees/)

### 3. Fake urgency and scarcity — widespread, measured, enforceable

Princeton's crawl of ~53K product pages across ~11K shopping sites found 1,818 dark-pattern
instances (15 types), including 183 sites using outright deception: countdown timers that reset on
reload, low-stock messages generated on schedules. The FTC report singles out fake timers and
false "almost sold out" messages. The UK CMA forced Booking.com, Expedia, Agoda, Hotels.com,
ebookers, and trivago to stop these practices in 2019.
Confidence: high (3-0).
Sources: [Mathur et al., Dark Patterns at Scale](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1907.07032) ·
[FTC dark-patterns report](https://www.ftc.gov/reports/bringing-dark-patterns-light)

### 4. Fareportal (CheapOair/OneTravel) — the travel-specific dark-patterns precedent

The New York AG's **$2.6M settlement** (March 2022) documented that Fareportal programmatically
fabricated scarcity: the "tickets left at this price" counter was set to the searched quantity
plus one (search 1 ticket → "Only 2 tickets left"), and popularity/social-proof counters (current
viewers, insurance purchases) were generated by random-number code.
Confidence: high (3-0).
Sources: [NY AG press release](https://ag.ny.gov/press-release/2022/attorney-general-james-secures-26-million-online-travel-agency-deceptive) ·
[executed AOD (PDF)](https://ag.ny.gov/sites/default/files/2022.03.16_nyag-fareportal_aod_fully_executed.pdf) ·
[Princeton CITP commentary](https://blog.citp.princeton.edu/2022/03/21/holding-purveyors-of-dark-patterns-for-online-travel-bookings-accountable/)

### 5. Kiwi.com — refund withholding as a signature failure

BBB's Miami profile records a formal complaint pattern: airlines refunded COVID-cancelled trips to
Kiwi.com, and Kiwi.com did not forward the money (PhocusWire reported 114 BBB complaints, 99
unanswered, F rating, Feb 2021). Newer complaints: forced cancel-and-rebook instead of date
changes, and Kiwi's own service fees excluded from refunded amounts.
Confidence: medium (BBB blocks automated fetching; verified via search index + corroboration).
Sources: [BBB Kiwi.com complaints](https://www.bbb.org/us/fl/miami/profile/travel-agency/kiwicom-inc-0633-90562676/complaints) ·
[PhocusWire](https://www.phocuswire.com/Kiwi-draws-complaints-over-refunds)

### 6. Obstructed cancellation — an active FTC enforcement priority

The FTC has sued companies for "requiring users to navigate a maze of screens in order to cancel."
Enforcement continues under ROSCA/Section 5 even after the click-to-cancel rule was vacated on
procedural grounds (July 2025) — see the $2.5B Amazon Prime settlement (Sept 2025). Any
subscription travel product (deal alerts, club membership) must make cancellation as easy as
sign-up.
Confidence: high (3-0).
Source: [FTC dark-patterns report](https://www.ftc.gov/reports/bringing-dark-patterns-light)

### 7. Membership travel clubs — the industry's worst fraud record

In April 2026 a federal court ordered a top operator of the "Consumer Law Protection"
timeshare-exit scheme to pay **$140 million** ($95M redress + $45M penalty) over a scheme that
took $90M+ from mostly older adults ($5,000–$80,000+ per person) via false affiliations,
unfulfilled refund promises, and "non-cancelable" contracts that violated the FTC Cooling-Off
Rule's 3-day cancellation right.
Confidence: high (3-0).
Source: [FTC press release](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2026/04/court-orders-operator-timeshare-exit-scheme-pay-140-million-related-ftc-allegations-scheme-took)

### 8. The FTC's standing red-flag list for vacation clubs

Per FTC consumer guidance: free-vacation lures into high-pressure presentations; "act today"
urgency (a per-se red flag — the company sets its own rates, so the deal should exist tomorrow);
enrollment fees where members *still* pay per stay; and the term "vacation club" used so
inconsistently that buyers must demand written, specific benefits.
Confidence: high (3-0).
Source: [FTC: Timeshares, vacation clubs, and related scams](https://consumer.ftc.gov/articles/timeshares-vacation-clubs-and-related-scams)

### 9. Enforcement now cascades into class actions

Junk-fee class actions more than doubled in 2024 vs 2023 filings; FTC/state-AG actions function as
templates that plaintiffs' firms replicate across competitors. Compliance is litigation insurance.
Confidence: medium (statistic traces to a single defense-firm publication).
Source: [Holland & Knight](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/04/the-rise-of-junk-fee-class-actions)

## Part 2 — The four named clubs (arrivia, RSI, Global Discovery, Westgate)

*(Direct-fetch limitations: BBB and Trustpilot blocked page loads; figures below come from
search-index excerpts of those pages and should be re-verified before publication.)*

### arrivia (arrivia.com) — the B2B platform layer

Scottsdale, AZ; formerly International Cruise & Excursions (ICE). White-label travel-loyalty
platform: partners brand an arrivia-run portal; arrivia supplies closed-user-group inventory and
keeps the margin spread ("partners choose how much of the savings to pass on"). Clients include
American Express, USAA, Marriott Vacation Club, and Westgate
([PR Newswire](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/westgate-resorts-extends-partnership-with-arrivia-and-expands-benefits-and-offerings-for-timeshare-owners-302117256.html)).
~$1.56B gross bookings 2024, #19 on
[Travel Weekly's 2025 Power List](https://www.travelweekly.com/Power-List-2025/Arrivia).

- **BBB:** A+ and accredited, yet ~147 complaints closed in 3 years
  ([BBB profile](https://www.bbb.org/us/az/scottsdale/profile/travel-club/arrivia-inc-1126-36004895)).
- **Consumer reviews:** Trustpilot "Poor" ~2.1/5; complaints center on undelivered partner-presentation
  vouchers, refund refusals, and add-on fees that erode advertised discounts
  ([Trustpilot](https://www.trustpilot.com/review/www.arrivia.com)).
- **Regulatory:** no direct FTC/AG action found (complaints often surface under client brand names,
  which suppresses attribution).
- **Verdict:** operationally credible at enterprise scale; consumer-facing fulfillment and fee
  transparency are the weak flank; white-labeling diffuses accountability.

### RSI Vacations (rsivacations.com)

Asheville, NC. Publicly positions as pure B2B fulfillment "behind 300+ brands" ("customers never
see RSI"), but complaints describe memberships sold by third-party marketers at home shows and
Zoom seminars — pitched at "$20/month" with a **~$10,300 deposit** in the contract, plus $100–$180
booking fees ([BBB complaints](https://www.bbb.org/us/nc/asheville/profile/travel-club/rsi-vacations-0473-92006465/complaints),
[ComplaintsBoard](https://www.complaintsboard.com/reservation-services-international-rsi-c500195)).

- **BBB:** A+, accredited only since May 2025; complaint count unverified (page blocked). Standard
  defense in responses: "RSI does not conduct sales seminars."
- **Reviews:** Trustpilot ~2.2/5; PissedConsumer 1.1/5. RSI's self-claimed "4.8★ Google / 4,000+
  reviews" is unverified.
- **Regulatory:** Connecticut DCP investigated and referred subpoena non-compliance to the state AG
  (2013; outcome unverified)
  ([CT DCP](https://portal.ct.gov/DCP/News-Releases-from-the-Department-of-Consumer-Protection/2013-News-Releases/Consumers-Cautioned-to-Beware-Travel-and-Timeshare-Schemes)).
  Note: "RSI, LLC" (Missouri timeshare-exit firm sued by the MO AG) is a **different company**.
- **Verdict:** real fulfillment backend; the arm's-length distributor model lets it keep an A+ BBB
  while disclaiming the sales conduct that generates most complaints.

### Global Discovery Vacations (globaldiscoveryvacations.com)

Consumer club of Global Connections, Inc. (Overland Park, KS), sold via prize-mailer-driven
presentations through "GVN" distributor companies. Upfront memberships to ~$8,000 plus ~$398
annual dues ([WFTV Action 9](https://www.wftv.com/news/action9/it-was-horrible-couple-claim-they-were-blindsided-by-vacation-club-fees/CJBF7SQRYBHA3JMUEFTRFOM6LU/)).

- **BBB:** Global Connections is A+ accredited, ~42 complaints in 3 years
  ([BBB](https://www.bbb.org/us/ks/overland-park/profile/travel-club/global-connections-inc-0714-97030227)).
- **Complaints:** availability failures ("nothing available" even a year out), savings that don't
  survive comparison ("often under $5" vs Hotels.com), undisclosed financing, cancellation
  difficulty ([ComplaintsBoard](https://www.complaintsboard.com/global-discovery-vacations-b121999)).
  Some genuinely satisfied long-term members exist
  ([TUG forum](https://tugbbs.com/forums/threads/global-discovery-vacations-good-or-bad.276238/)).
- **Regulatory — the most substantial record of the four:** Ohio AG suit (2014, Prize Rule and CSPA
  violations, breached 2010 compliance assurance)
  ([coverage](https://americajr.com/news/2014/10/08/ohio-attorney-general-dewine-announces-lawsuit-against-global-vacation-network/));
  Illinois AG **$250,000 settlement** (2019) mandating recorded verification calls and a 7-day
  cancellation right
  ([IL AG settlement PDF](https://illinoisattorneygeneral.gov/dA/a419d4fc6e/201911-14%20SETTLEMENT%20WITH%20COMPANIES%20SELLING%20TRAVEL%20CLUB%20MEMBERSHIPS.pdf));
  Florida AG settlement (practices changed, wrongdoing denied)
  ([WFTV](https://www.wftv.com/news/action9/it-was-horrible-couple-claim-they-were-blindsided-by-vacation-club-fees/CJBF7SQRYBHA3JMUEFTRFOM6LU/)).
- **Verdict:** real condos, some happy members — undermined by distributor-driven deceptive selling
  (3 state AG actions) and persistent availability failures.

### WG Travel Club (wgtravelclub.com) — actually Westgate Travel Club

Westgate Resorts' membership product (not an MLM; no evidence of network marketing), sold at
timeshare presentations to prospects who decline the timeshare. Tiers reportedly $2,000–$20,000
(e.g., ~$5,520: ~$1,932 deposit + $230/month), $199 annual renewal, plus booking charges
([PissedConsumer](https://westgate-travel-club.pissedconsumer.com/review.html),
[Westgate T&C](https://www.westgateresorts.com/travel-club-tc/)). Its Cruise & Travel Collection
is **powered by arrivia** — club #4 on this list is a client of club #1
([PR Newswire](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/westgate-resorts-extends-partnership-with-arrivia-and-expands-benefits-and-offerings-for-timeshare-owners-302117256.html)).

- **BBB:** no standalone Travel Club profile found; complaints roll up to Westgate Resorts /
  Central Florida Investments. Westgate Resorts overall: 1.3/5 on
  [Trustpilot](https://www.trustpilot.com/review/westgateresorts.com).
- **Complaints:** "pricing comparable or worse than public travel sites"; promo credits worth "a few
  dollars per booking"; advertised "$99 getaways never available"; rescission windows not disclosed;
  blackout dates despite "no blackout" claims
  ([PissedConsumer](https://westgate-travel-club.pissedconsumer.com/review.html),
  [ComplaintsBoard](https://www.complaintsboard.com/westgate-travel-club-deceptive-dealings-c390900)).
- **Regulatory:** none found against the Travel Club product itself; parent Westgate has an
  extensive timeshare-sales litigation history.
- **Verdict:** legitimate arrivia-powered inventory sold with timeshare-style pressure, delivering
  discounts members repeatedly find they can beat on public OTAs.

### The structural weakness of the closed-user-group club model

All four share one defect: **they charge non-refundable money upfront for savings that cannot be
verified at purchase time — and that frequently don't survive comparison shopping afterward.**

1. **Opaque pricing vs public OTAs.** Members-only walls prevent pre-purchase comparison; post-purchase,
   members find club rates beaten by Booking/Hotels.com
   ([SmarterTravel](https://www.smartertravel.com/how-can-you-tell-if-a-travel-club-is-legit/)).
2. **Upfront fees invert the risk.** The member must out-travel a $2K–$20K sunk cost; state AGs warn
   most never break even
   ([Michigan AG alert](https://www.michigan.gov/consumerprotection/protect-yourself/consumer-alerts/shopping/travel-club-memberships),
   [NC DOJ](https://ncdoj.gov/protecting-consumers/travel/travel-clubs/)).
3. **Slippery savings claims.** "Save 50%" means "up to 50%"; availability restrictions gut the value.
4. **Accountability diffusion by design.** Platform → distributor → member lets each layer blame the
   other — which is why **A+ BBB ratings coexist with 1–2 star reviews** across all four (BBB grades
   reward complaint-answering; the sales layer generating complaints sits outside the rated entity).

## Part 3 — What the best-regarded players do right

*Claims tagged **[FACT]** (published policy, guarantee, official stat) or **[SENTIMENT]**
(review/forum consensus, not independently verified).*

### Costco Travel
- **All-in, no-surprise pricing**: rental-car quotes include taxes, airport/facility, and concession
  fees upfront; package pages show total price + included extras before checkout. **[FACT]**
  ([upgradedpoints](https://upgradedpoints.com/travel/costco-travel-review/))
- **Free included extras**: free additional driver with Alamo/Enterprise/Avis/Budget; free online
  cancellation on most car reservations. **[FACT]**
  ([Costco Travel FAQ](https://www.costcotravel.com/Info/Rental-Cars-FAQs))
- **Executive 2% reward** applies to travel (up to $1,000/yr back). **[FACT]**
- Forum consensus: "same price + extra perks" — e.g., identical Disney Cruise price plus a $350 shop
  card; praised phone support. **[SENTIMENT]** ([theeverymom](https://theeverymom.com/costco-travel/))

### Google Flights
- **No booking fees; direct handoff** to airline/OTA — Google is not the merchant. **[FACT]**
  ([Google support](https://support.google.com/travel/answer/9430556))
- **Price Guarantee**: on badged US-departing itineraries, fare drops >$5 are auto-refunded, up to
  $500/account/year (badge still rare). **[FACT]**
- **Pay-to-rank disclaimed**: partners don't pay to appear; ranking not influenced by partner
  relationships; ~300+ partners compared. **[FACT]**
  ([Google support](https://support.google.com/travel/answer/7664728?hl=en))
- Displayed fare ≈ checkout fare is the accuracy benchmark reviewers measure others against. **[SENTIMENT]**

### Going (formerly Scott's Cheap Flights)
- **One deal pays for the year**: $49/yr Premium (Elite $199), cancel anytime; 40–90%-off alerts.
  **[FACT]** on pricing; payback claim **[SENTIMENT]**
  ([CNBC Select](https://www.cnbc.com/select/scotts-cheap-flights-review/))
- **"Excellent" on Trustpilot** under going.com (legacy scottscheapflights.com listing skews
  negative — deals gone by send time). **[SENTIMENT]**
  ([Trustpilot](https://www.trustpilot.com/review/going.com))
- **Transparent free-vs-paid tiers** make the upgrade decision low-pressure. **[SENTIMENT]**

### Kayak / Skyscanner
- **Skyscanner "no hidden fees" pledge**: displayed prices include estimated taxes/charges; revenue
  is referral commission, not user markups. **[FACT]**
  ([Skyscanner help](https://help.skyscanner.net/hc/en-us/articles/201208841-What-do-you-include-in-the-prices-shown-on-Skyscanner-))
- **Kayak Price Forecast publishes a confidence level** and measures accuracy daily (~85% per
  third-party review). **[FACT]** on design; usefulness **[SENTIMENT]**
  ([Kayak](https://www.kayak.com/price-trend-explanation))
- Both honestly caveat that results aren't exhaustive and click-through prices can differ. **[FACT]**

### Booking.com
- **Unmatched inventory**: ~4.4M properties end-2025; most-visited travel site (~560M visits/mo).
  **[FACT]** ([news.booking.com](https://news.booking.com/fast-facts/))
- **Free cancellation as a first-class filter** — the repeatedly cited retention driver despite
  service complaints. **[SENTIMENT]**
- **Genius loyalty with real, automatic discounts**: 100M+ members; Level 2–3 members are ~30% of
  actives but >50% of room nights; only properties rated ≥7.5 can join. **[FACT]**
  ([hospitality.today](https://www.hospitality.today/article/genius-members-drive-over-half-of-booking-com-bookings))
- **Reviews tied to real bookings**: 70M+, only bookers can review, fake-review detection.
  **[FACT]** on policy (caveat: verifies the booking, not the stay).

### Hopper (despite the FTC settlement)
- **4.8/5 from 1M+ App Store ratings** (notably lower ~3.8 on Google Play); praised color-coded
  price calendar. **[FACT]** on ratings
- **Price Freeze fills a real gap**: lock a fare for a small fee; Hopper covers rises up to $300.
  **[FACT]** on terms; satisfaction **[SENTIMENT]**
- Its "95% accurate predictions" claim is self-reported and unverified; third-party tests found it
  solid but missing 10–15% of late fare drops. **[UNVERIFIED CLAIM]**

### AAA Travel
- **Human agents included in membership** (most agencies charge consult fees). **[FACT]**
  ([AAA](https://www.aaa.com/tripcanvas/article/aaa-travel-agent-can-save-you-time-and-money-CM280))
- **Member rates below OTAs**: 36+ hotel-brand partnerships, up to 20% off car-rental base rates +
  free child seat, published best-price guarantee. **[FACT]**
- One or two trips/year in discounts typically covers the membership; roadside-assistance trust
  halo with older travelers. **[SENTIMENT]**

### The top 5 trust-building mechanics (ranked by recurrence across the seven)

1. **All-in / what-you-see-is-what-you-pay pricing** (5 of 7) — the single most repeated praise theme.
2. **Price-drop protection / fare-lock products** (4 of 7) — Google's guarantee, Hopper's freeze,
   Kayak's confidence-scored forecasts, Going's framed savings.
3. **No-fee handoff / commission-not-markup model made explicit** (4 of 7) — "we don't mark up, we
   don't sell ranking" stated as policy.
4. **Free cancellation as a default, low-friction option** (3 of 7).
5. **Human support included in the price** (3 of 7).

**Cross-cutting takeaway:** the winners don't necessarily have the lowest price — they remove
*ambush risk*. Every top-rated player converts a normally hidden cost (taxes, fees, fare
volatility, cancellation penalties, ranking bias) into an explicit, published commitment. The two
membership models that work (Costco, AAA) sell trust they already earned elsewhere — they never
ask you to pre-pay for unverifiable savings.

## Part 4 — The 10 lessons for THE Travel Club (ranked by evidence recurrence)

1. **Show all-in prices from the first screen; never drip mandatory fees.** (6+ independent evidence
   streams: FTC report, Junk Fees Rule, Hopper BBB pattern + $35M settlement, Fareportal, class-action surge.)
2. **Never fabricate urgency, scarcity, or social proof.** No countdown timers, "only N left," or
   viewer counts unless they are real. (FTC, Princeton, Fareportal N+1, UK CMA.)
3. **Pass supplier refunds through fully and fast; never keep service fees out of refundable amounts.**
   (Kiwi.com BBB pattern, Hopper BBB pattern, timeshare refund failures.)
4. **Make cancellation exactly as easy as sign-up.** (FTC enforcement priority; Amazon $2.5B;
   Cooling-Off violations in the $140M judgment.)
5. **Never pre-select paid add-ons; require express consent for every charge.** (The core of
   Hopper's $35M settlement.)
6. **Never use "buy today or lose the deal" pressure in membership sales.** (FTC per-se red flag;
   central to the $140M case and three state-AG actions against Global Connections.)
7. **Define membership benefits precisely, in writing.** "Travel club" has no fixed meaning — the
   trust gap THE Travel Club can exploit by being explicit.
8. **Honor cooling-off/cancellation rights scrupulously; never claim contracts are non-cancelable.**
9. **Staff reachable, human customer service that owns problems.** "We're bound by provider
   policies" is the defense that failed Hopper and arrivia alike.
10. **Treat pricing compliance as litigation insurance.** Every enforcement action becomes a
    plaintiffs'-bar template; exceeding the FTC fee rule is cheaper than defending copycat suits.

### How the current engine already maps to these lessons

| Lesson | Engine capability (already shipped) |

| All-in prices (1) | `{amount, total, base, taxes, fees, estimated}`; ranking uses `total`; `priceComparable` refuses to overclaim |
| No fake urgency (2) | No scarcity/urgency fields exist anywhere in the response contract |
| Honest freshness (2, 7) | `live` / `cached` / `demo` labels; demo data can never masquerade as live |
| Honest failures (9) | "Sources temporarily unavailable" vs "no matches"; provider error categories |

### The strategic wedge

The incumbent club model hides prices behind a paywall and gets caught overcharging. THE Travel
Club's architecture does the opposite: it shows the public comparison, flags estimates honestly,
and never asks anyone to pre-pay for unverifiable savings. **"The travel club that shows you the
public price next to ours"** is a pitch none of the incumbents above can make — their business
model forbids it.
